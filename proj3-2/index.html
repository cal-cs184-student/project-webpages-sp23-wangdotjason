Explain how these bounce numbers relate to the particular effects that appear. Make sure to include all screenshots.

1. With a ray max depth of 1, only direct bounces into the viewpoint are rendered. This includes the walls, floor, and reflection off the balls.
The ceiling is not rendered because it is in the same plane as the light source so it is impossible for light to reach it in only a single bounce.

2. With a max ray depth of 2, the ceiling and shadows becomes illuminated because light can bounce off objects back into the ceiling/shadow. For this same reason, the
direct reflection off both balls are rendered. The glass ball appears black because refractions inside the ball have to hit another side of the ball one more
time before they can escape.

3. With a max ray depth of 3, the glass ball becomes transparent because the refraction have entered the ball, exited the ball, and hit the environment/other ball.
For this same reason reason the shadow underneath the glass ball becomes illuminated as the light refracts twice through the ball, hitting the floor

4. With a max ray depth of 4, the underside of the glass ball is illuminated as the light that refracted through the ball and hit the ground bounced
back up into the underside of the glass ball. The reflection of the glass ball also becomes transparent for the same reason as the previous part, only
that the light had to be bounced off the metal ball first.

5. With a max ray depth of 5, there is not a significant difference because the magnitude of any ray that hits a non mirror surface is very low.
The only rays that persist after 5 bounces are those bounced between mirror surfaces, which account for only a couple rays at this resolution,
thus not making a huge impact on the final image

6. With a max ray depth of 100, there is once again not a huge difference between previous renders as the majority of rays have been nullified. The glints
on top of the glass ball appear as a result of indirect light reflecting off the top of the glass ball with a low probablity, which is why it does not
appear very distinctly on previous images.

The alpha value represents the roughness of the material so in the first image when there's a low alpha value, the dragon is much glossier/specular, which causes more specularity in the image.
As the alpha value increases, reflections off the dragon are more diffuse, creating a matte surface and a "cleaner" looking image because the light is more evenly dispersed into the environment.
Furthermore, the shadows in the areas of the dragon not directly in the light become brighter as the diffusion of light illuminates those areas more brightly.


Show two images of scene `CBbunny_microfacet_cu.dae` rendered using cosine hemisphere sampling (default) and your importance sampling. The sampling rate should be fixed at 64 samples per pixel and 1 samples per light. The number of bounces should be at least 5. Briefly discuss their difference.

importance sampling


Importance sampling directs the light samples to cover our area of interest, which in this case is the bunny. Cosine hemisphere sampling sends samples in a hemisphere all throughot the environment, with on a small fraction of them
hitting the bunny. This is why for the same number of light samples, the bunny rendered with importance sampling looks more complete. With an infinite number of light samples, both methods would produce identical results.
